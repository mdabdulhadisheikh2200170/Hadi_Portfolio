The Case for Physics-Informed Loss Functions in Environmental ML

MSE is the wrong tool for physical systems. A short argument for treating conservation laws as first-class citizens in the loss function.

2023.08.19

Published

5 min

Read Time

Theory

Beyond MSE

Standard Mean Squared Error treats every prediction error as equally bad. A 5% overestimate of a solute concentration and a prediction of negative concentration contribute proportionally to the loss. That's fine for photo classification. It's absurd for environmental systems where "impossible" and "slightly wrong" should not be adjacent in loss space.

Physics-informed loss functions correct this by making physically impossible predictions catastrophically expensive. The optimizer learns to avoid entire regions of parameter space that violate known laws — not because the data forbids them, but because the loss itself does.

Water Balance as Constraint

Every closed hydrological system obeys the same accounting identity: Inflow − Outflow = ΔStorage. If a model predicts that 10 mm of water entered a catchment and 15 mm left it with no storage change, the model is wrong regardless of what the calibration error suggests.

Embedding water balance as an explicit loss term forces the model to respect this identity across every training batch. The result is often a small drop in point-wise accuracy in exchange for a large improvement in aggregate behavior — a trade almost always worth making in operational settings.

Darcy's Law Integration

For groundwater systems, Darcy's law (q = −K∇h) provides a differentiable constraint that connects predicted heads to predicted fluxes. Embedding this relationship as a soft loss term guides the optimizer toward parameter configurations where predicted flows and predicted heads are internally consistent.

Even sparse training data becomes more informative under this constraint, because the physics term propagates information across the domain in ways pure regression cannot.

Implementation Pattern

The general pattern looks like this:

Ltotal = Ldata + α · Lphysics + β · Lboundary

The weighting schedule matters more than the initial weights. In practice, start with a high α to establish physics-consistent representations, then anneal it downward so the data can drive fine-tuning within that constrained space. Fixed weights almost always underperform a schedule.

When Not to Use

Physics-informed losses are not free. They add computational cost, complicate hyperparameter tuning, and can over-constrain models in highly heterogeneous or poorly characterized systems. If your "physics" is really a rough approximation of processes you don't fully understand, hardcoding it into the loss can bias the model away from patterns the data would otherwise reveal.

The honest rule of thumb: use physics-informed losses when your physics confidence exceeds your data confidence. If it's the other way around, let the data speak first.
